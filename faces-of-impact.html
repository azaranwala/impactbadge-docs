Day 1
Environment
S

Sarah, 16 — Richmond, VA

Junior at Deep Run High School

"I signed up because my counselor said I needed 80 hours. I stayed because I realized I could actually see the river getting cleaner."
Sarah didn't care about volunteer hours. She cared about getting into UVA. When her counselor told her she needed 80 hours for the CSL diploma seal, she groaned and signed up for the first event she found on ImpactBadge — a river cleanup with the Alliance for the Chesapeake Bay. She showed up that Saturday expecting to pick up a few bottles and leave. Instead, she spent four hours pulling a shopping cart out of the James River. "I was so mad that someone dumped that there," she says. "But then I looked at the pile of trash we'd collected and thought — we actually did something today." That was 14 months ago. Sarah has now logged 127 hours across 23 events. She leads her school's environmental club, organized three park cleanups, and earned the "50hr Master" badge on ImpactBadge. Her graduation progress bar is deep green — 127 of 80 hours. "The hours stopped mattering after about month two. Now I just check ImpactBadge to see if there's something this weekend." She exported her hours to Common App last week. Under "Activity Description," she wrote: "Removed 2,400 lbs of debris from Richmond waterways." That's not a number she made up — ImpactBadge tracked it.

Marcus, 14 — Washington, DC

Freshman at Cardozo Education Campus

"My family used the food bank when I was little. Now I work there. The lady at the counter recognized me and started crying."
Marcus doesn't talk about the years his family needed help. But he remembers the woman at the food pantry who always gave him an extra box of cereal. "She'd wink and say 'growing boys need fuel,'" he recalls. When DC announced that all students need 100 community service hours to graduate, Marcus knew exactly where he'd go. He found FeedMore's youth volunteer program on ImpactBadge and signed up for a Saturday shift. His first day, he packed 200 meal kits. His second day, the woman from his childhood was still there. She recognized him. "She grabbed my hands and said, 'Look at you. Look at you helping now.' I couldn't even talk." Marcus has logged 38 hours in his first semester. He goes every Saturday. His mom follows his progress on the ImpactBadge parent dashboard. "She screenshots my badges and sends them to my grandmother," he laughs. He's already thinking about starting a food drive at his school. "100 hours isn't enough. I'll probably do 300."

Senior at Digital Harbor Foundation

"I failed algebra twice. Now I tutor algebra. Life is weird like that."
Jordan almost didn't graduate. He failed algebra in 9th grade, repeated it in 10th, and barely passed in 11th. "Math just didn't click for me until Mr. Rodriguez explained it using basketball stats," he says. That experience changed how Jordan sees education. When Maryland's 75-hour service learning requirement loomed, he chose to tutor middle schoolers at the IRC refugee youth program. "These kids are learning math in a language they just started speaking. If I can learn algebra after failing twice, they can definitely learn it." He uses ImpactBadge to log every tutoring session. His supervisor at IRC verifies the hours via email — no ImpactBadge account needed. "I just get an email, click verify, done," the supervisor says. Jordan's portfolio shows 89 hours, 3 reflections about his tutoring experience, and a recommendation from IRC. He exported it all to Common App last month. "My college essay is about failing algebra. The activities section is about teaching it. I think admissions will see the growth." He got early acceptance to Towson University. Full ride.
